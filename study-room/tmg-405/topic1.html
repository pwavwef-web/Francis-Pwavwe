The Nature and Purpose of Visitor Attractions in the Tourism System

Understanding what visitor attractions are, how they are constituted, why they matter, and how they are managed within the broader tourism system.

üìå 1.1 Definitions of Visitor Attractions

Defining a visitor attraction is more complex than it first appears. The term embraces a hugely diverse range of places, experiences, and features, yet scholars and practitioners have proposed several working definitions:

Swarbrooke (1995): "A visitor attraction is a feature of an area that attracts visitors and may be managed to provide facilities and services for them."
Leiper (1990): A visitor attraction is a system comprising three elements ‚Äî a tourist or human element, a nucleus or central element (the feature that draws people), and a marker or informative element that informs tourists about the nucleus.
Middleton (1994): "A permanent resource, either natural or man-made, which is developed and managed for the primary purpose of attracting visitors."

Common to most definitions are three key ideas:

  • There is a site or place ‚Äî something tangible or intangible that draws people
  • It motivates travel ‚Äî it is the reason (or one of the reasons) a person decides to visit
  • It is, or can be, managed ‚Äî it can be planned, developed, and maintained

It is important to distinguish between primary attractions (the main reason people travel to a destination), secondary attractions (those visited incidentally), and support services such as accommodation and transport that facilitate but do not directly motivate travel.

üß© 1.2 Components of a Visitor Attraction

A visitor attraction is rarely a single, undifferentiated entity. Most scholars identify several interrelated components:

The Core Product (Nucleus)

The central feature that motivates a visit ‚Äî a natural landmark, a historic monument, a theme park ride, or a cultural event. Without a compelling nucleus there is no attraction.

Physical Infrastructure

Car parks, pathways, signage, visitor centres, toilets, catering outlets, and retail. These do not motivate travel but are essential to sustaining the visit experience and generating revenue.

Services and Programming

Guided tours, interpretive panels, educational workshops, events, and entertainment. Services transform the passive viewing of a nucleus into an active, memorable experience.

Human Capital

Staff, guides, and volunteers who deliver customer service, provide interpretation, and ensure visitor safety. People are often the most critical quality variable in service delivery.

Management and Governance

The ownership, organisational structures, policies, and strategies that govern how the attraction operates ‚Äî encompassing financial management, marketing, human resources, and sustainability planning.

Collectively these components form the attraction system. A weakness in any one component can undermine the entire visitor experience.

üéØ 1.3 Purpose of a Visitor Attraction

Visitor attractions serve multiple stakeholders simultaneously, and their purposes are therefore multifaceted:

Economic Purpose

  • Generate direct revenue through admissions, retail, catering, and events
  • Create employment ‚Äî both direct (on-site staff) and indirect (supply chain)
  • Stimulate broader regional economic activity by attracting visitors who spend money across accommodation, transport, and local services
  • Act as catalysts for urban or rural regeneration

Social and Cultural Purpose

  • Conserve and interpret natural, cultural, and heritage resources for present and future generations
  • Promote cultural exchange and cross-cultural understanding between visitors and host communities
  • Provide educational opportunities for school groups, researchers, and lifelong learners
  • Strengthen community identity and pride

Environmental Purpose

  • Generate funds for conservation (e.g. national parks using entry fees for habitat management)
  • Raise environmental awareness through on-site interpretation and education
  • Support the rationale for protecting natural areas from development

Destination Marketing Purpose

  • Serve as the primary motivators that differentiate one destination from another
  • Anchor the destination's image and brand identity
  • Provide content for destination marketing campaigns

‚öôÔ∏è 1.4 The Concept of Visitor Attraction Management

Visitor attraction management refers to the systematic process of planning, organising, directing, and controlling all aspects of an attraction to achieve its objectives while delivering high-quality visitor experiences.

Key management domains include:

  • Strategic Management: Setting the mission, vision, and long-term goals of the attraction; conducting market analysis and competitive positioning
  • Operations Management: Day-to-day running of the attraction ‚Äî opening hours, staffing rotas, maintenance schedules, safety procedures, and crowd control
  • Marketing Management: Identifying target markets, positioning the attraction, and communicating its value proposition through appropriate channels
  • Financial Management: Budgeting, pricing strategies, revenue diversification, and financial reporting
  • Human Resource Management: Recruitment, training, motivation, and retention of staff and volunteers
  • Visitor Management: Techniques to distribute visitors through space and time to minimise negative environmental and social impacts while maximising satisfaction
  • Sustainability Management: Balancing economic viability, social responsibility, and environmental stewardship
Swarbrooke's integrated management model argues that effective attraction management requires the simultaneous pursuit of visitor satisfaction, staff wellbeing, commercial viability, and resource conservation ‚Äî four goals that can sometimes be in tension with one another.

The management context of a visitor attraction is also shaped by its ownership type: public sector attractions (e.g. national museums) are guided by policy objectives; private-sector commercial attractions prioritise profit; not-for-profit trusts (e.g. heritage charities) balance income generation with conservation mandates; and community-owned attractions serve local development goals.
