Classifications of Visitor Attractions

How visitor attractions are categorised using different typologies, and an analysis of the visitor attraction as a product.

üóÇÔ∏è 2.1 Typologies of Visitor Attractions

A typology is a system of classification that groups attractions by shared characteristics. Several frameworks have been proposed, and each reveals a different dimension of the attraction universe.

A. Natural vs. Built (Man-Made) Attractions

Perhaps the most fundamental distinction is between natural and built attractions:

  • Natural attractions ‚Äî features of the physical environment: mountains, waterfalls, beaches, caves, wildlife, national parks. Their appeal often derives from their scenic beauty, biodiversity, or rarity.
  • Built attractions ‚Äî created by human activity. These divide into:
    • Purpose-built: theme parks, zoos, aquaria, heritage centres, casinos
    • Incidentally attractive: buildings or sites originally constructed for other purposes that now attract visitors ‚Äî cathedrals, castles, industrial heritage sites

Many attractions combine both dimensions: a wetland bird sanctuary may have a natural core with a purpose-built visitor centre and boardwalk infrastructure.

B. Swarbrooke's Four-Category Typology

Swarbrooke (1995) proposed a widely cited classification with four broad categories:

  1. Natural attractions: beaches, cliffs, forests, rivers, lakes, wildlife
  2. Man-made attractions not originally built to attract tourists: cathedrals, stately homes, industrial museums, archaeological sites
  3. Man-made attractions built to attract tourists: theme parks, heritage centres, purpose-built museums, resorts
  4. Special events: festivals, sporting events, carnivals, trade fairs ‚Äî temporary in nature but powerful motivators of travel

C. Classification by Ownership / Management Sector

  • Public sector: national parks, state museums, government-run historic sites ‚Äî typically subsidised, with social and educational objectives
  • Private sector (commercial): theme parks, private safari lodges, commercial heritage properties ‚Äî primarily profit-driven
  • Voluntary / Not-for-Profit: charitable trusts, community heritage groups, religious organisations ‚Äî motivated by conservation or community benefit

D. Classification by Visitor Access and Scale

  • Free vs. charged entry: some attractions rely on retail, catering, and donations rather than admission fees
  • Regional vs. national vs. international draw: a local craft market draws regional visitors; an iconic world heritage site attracts an international market
  • Permanent vs. temporary: permanent facilities vs. seasonal, event-based attractions

E. Thematic Classifications

Some typologies organise attractions by their primary theme or experiential content:

  • Heritage attractions: historic buildings, battlefields, archaeological sites, living history museums
  • Cultural attractions: art galleries, theatres, ethnic neighbourhoods, craft villages
  • Natural/Eco attractions: national parks, wildlife reserves, botanical gardens
  • Leisure and entertainment attractions: theme parks, casinos, sports stadia, water parks
  • Industrial attractions: factory tours, mine museums, open-air industrial heritage
  • Religious / Pilgrimage attractions: cathedrals, shrines, sacred sites
Note: No single typology is definitive. In practice, managers and researchers often use multiple classification dimensions simultaneously ‚Äî for instance, describing an attraction as natural, internationally significant, free-entry, and conservation-oriented.

üì¶ 2.2 The Visitor Attraction Product

Marketing theory distinguishes between different levels of a product. Applied to visitor attractions, the visitor attraction product can be analysed at several layers:

A. Core, Actual, and Augmented Product (Kotler)

  • Core product: the fundamental benefit the visitor seeks ‚Äî entertainment, education, relaxation, spiritual fulfilment, adventure. At its most abstract, visitors buy an experience or memory, not a physical object.
  • Actual (tangible) product: the physical components and services that deliver the core benefit ‚Äî the exhibits, the rides, the guided tour, the site itself.
  • Augmented product: additional services and attributes that exceed basic expectations ‚Äî exceptional customer service, free Wi-Fi, mobile apps, loyalty programmes, accessibility provisions.

B. Service Characteristics of the Attraction Product

As a service product, the attraction experience has four key characteristics (the IHIP model):

  • Intangibility: the experience cannot be inspected or sampled before purchase. Visitors must rely on marketing communications, reviews, and word-of-mouth.
  • Heterogeneity (variability): each visitor's experience is unique ‚Äî shaped by weather, crowd levels, staff quality, and personal mood. Quality management is therefore challenging.
  • Inseparability: production and consumption occur simultaneously. The visitor must be physically present; the experience is produced and consumed at the same moment.
  • Perishability: unsold visitor slots (a queue that never materialised) cannot be stored. Capacity management and demand forecasting are critical.

C. The Attraction as an Experience Product

Beyond services, modern attraction management increasingly conceptualises the product as a designed experience. Pine and Gilmore's (1999) Experience Economy framework (covered in depth in Topic 4) argues that attractions can differentiate themselves by staging memorable, personalised experiences rather than simply delivering services.

This shifts the managerial focus from operational efficiency to experience design ‚Äî orchestrating all touchpoints to create emotional resonance, authenticity, and narrative coherence.

D. The Attraction Product Life Cycle

Like any product, visitor attractions follow a product life cycle ‚Äî introduction, growth, maturity, and decline (though some reach a stable plateau rather than declining). Management implications:

  • Introduction: heavy marketing spend, visitor numbers building, operating at a loss
  • Growth: rapid increase in visitors, revenue rises, competitors may enter
  • Maturity: visitor numbers stable but competitive pressure increases; management must innovate to sustain appeal
  • Decline: falling visitor numbers; options include refurbishment/reinvention, repositioning, or closure
Butler's TALC (Tourism Area Life Cycle, 1980) applies a similar framework at the destination level ‚Äî exploration, involvement, development, consolidation, stagnation, followed by possible rejuvenation or decline.
